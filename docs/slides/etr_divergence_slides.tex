  \item Treasury actual: \tier{T = 10.49\%} (Feb 2026), 9.88\% at Dec 2025.
  \item A persistent gap of \alert{3.6--5.2 pp} between the schedule on paper and the duties Treasury collects (S0$-$T including USMCA backstory: 6.1--7.8 pp).
  \item The gap is not noise. It reflects \alert{real behavioral and administrative responses}: USMCA claiming, supply-chain diversion, exemptions, timing.
  \item For policy and welfare analysis we need to know \alert{which channels carry the gap} and how they evolve.
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{What the literature has established}

\small

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Gopinath \& Neiman (2026, NBER w34620)} document the gap; pass-through on \emph{collected} duties is near 100\%, so the gap is what shields prices, not under-implementation.
  \item \textbf{Azzimonti (2025, Richmond Fed)} decomposes May 2025 into three pieces: within-country product mix (0.6 pp), cross-country share shifts (2.7 pp), implementation frictions (5.5 pp).
  \item \textbf{Eck, Hoang, Mix, Ray (2026, FEDS Notes ``Mind the Gap'')} find the December 2025 announced ETR was 14.7\%; realized fell short by 5.43 pp ($\approx$44\% of the announced increase). They split into composition and rate-discrepancy channels with Laspeyres baseline weights. \alert{Our framework recovers their numbers within 0.5 pp at every comparable quantity (Dec 2025: S1 = 15.07 vs 14.7; gap = 5.19 vs 5.43 pp); see appendix.}
  \item \textbf{PWBM (2025--26)} tracks the USMCA claim-rate ramp: $\sim$33\% (2024) $\to$ $\sim$88\% (Dec 2025) for CA/MX. Mechanical-vs-behavioral split shows $\sim$\$46B lost to behavior.
  \item \textbf{Waugh (2026, Minneapolis Fed)} computes a Trade Restrictiveness Index ($\sim$21--23\%) capturing the welfare cost of dispersion, $\approx$2$\times$ the duties-to-imports ratio.
\end{itemize}

Existing work is piecewise: each paper opens one channel. We unify them.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Our approach: a five-channel ladder}

\small

We construct a sequence of counterfactual ETRs and walk from a fully static baseline to the Treasury-observed rate. Each step turns one channel \emph{on}:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{r l l}
\toprule
\tier{S0}        & USMCA 2024 baseline shares       & $\times$ 2024 weights   \\
                 & \ldots\, \gap{$+$ USMCA adjustment} & (claim rates 2024 $\to$ H2-2025) \\
\tier{S1}        & USMCA H2-2025 baseline           & $\times$ 2024 weights   \\
                 & \ldots\, \gap{$+$ trade diversion}  & (composition shift)              \\
\tier{S2}        & USMCA H2-2025 baseline           & $\times$ monthly weights         \\
                 & \ldots\, \gap{$+$ all-other preferences} & (Annex II / ITA / Ch98 / KORUS / GSP)\\
\tier{S3}        & + non-USMCA preferences          & $\times$ monthly weights         \\
                 & \ldots\, \gap{$+$ residual + timing}  & (AVE failures, AD/CVD, refunds, ACH)  \\
\tier{T}         & Treasury actual ETR              &                                \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.5em}

\textbf{Two design choices:}
\begin{itemize}\small
  \item \textbf{S1 is the framework anchor}, not S0. By absorbing USMCA stabilization upfront we treat the 2024 $\to$ H2-2025 claim-rate ramp as backstory. Most analysis lives between S1 and T.
  \item \tier{S1} is the same line that anchors the headline figure (tracker daily ETR collapsed monthly). The framework backbone aligns with what economists already plot.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The math}

\small

For an aggregate ETR $S = \sum_i w_i r_i$ over $i = (\text{HS10}, \text{country})$ cells, every rung is a fixed-rate or fixed-weights move. The Shapley two-way decomposition along any partition $g$ (e.g., partner group, product group):

\[
\Delta S = \underbrace{\sum_g \tfrac{1}{2}(R_g^{0} + R_g^{1})\,(s_g^{0} - s_g^{1})}_{\text{between-group}} + \underbrace{\sum_g \tfrac{1}{2}(s_g^{0} + s_g^{1})\,(R_g^{0} - R_g^{1})}_{\text{within-group}}
\]

\begin{itemize}
  \item Both partitions (country / product) sum exactly to $\Delta S$ at every month — apples-to-apples comparable.
  \item \emph{Between} captures composition (which countries / products account for more or less of the trade basket); \emph{within} captures intra-group rate shifts.
  \item All four rate panels (\texttt{rate\_2024}, \texttt{rate\_h2avg}, \texttt{rate\_usmca\_monthly}, \texttt{rate\_all\_pref}) are produced by the same R day-weighting machinery against tracker scenario snapshots, so the rungs differ only in their \emph{stated} dimension (USMCA layer or weights), not in numerical method.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{S2 $\to$ S3: all-other preferences}

\small

Seven preference channels (importer-declared via IMDB), classified into three groups:
\begin{description}\small
  \item[Major IEEPA carve-outs] \texttt{duty\_free} entries (\texttt{rate\_prov} 10/18/19): Annex II, ITA, Chapter 98 / Berman Amendment, generic pharma. Reduces both MFN base \emph{and} IEEPA reciprocal — large swing for affected lines.
  \item[Bilateral preferences] KORUS, AU, IL, SG, CL, CO, PE, PA, JO, MA, OM, BH, JP. Reduces MFN base only.
  \item[GSP / AGOA] \texttt{A}, \texttt{A+}, \texttt{D}, \texttt{E}, \texttt{J}, \texttt{W}, \texttt{Z}, \texttt{N}. Reduces MFN base only.
\end{description}

\vspace{0.4em}

\textbf{Math:} per cell,
\[
\Delta_{\text{base}} = (s_{\text{duty\_free}} + s_{\text{korus}} + s_{\text{gsp}} + s_{\text{other\_fta}}) \cdot r_{\text{base}}^{\text{pre}}, \qquad \Delta_{\text{recip}} = s_{\text{duty\_free}} \cdot r_{\text{IEEPA}}^{\text{pre}}
\]
\[
r_{\text{S3}} = \max\!\left(0,\ r_{\text{S2}} - \Delta_{\text{base}} - \Delta_{\text{recip}}\right)
\]

The \texttt{duty\_free}-on-IEEPA term is the policy-salient piece: it captures executive-branch carve-outs from the reciprocal regime. KORUS/GSP/other-FTA are ``preferences as usual.''

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Investigation paths from here}

\small

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Convergence dynamics.} Eck et al.\ predict the gap compresses if tariffs persist (frontloading depletes; shipping lags resolve). Our monthly day-weighted panel can test this directly: does the residual rung shrink month-over-month conditional on stable rate panels?
  \item \textbf{Annex II quantification.} The largest single chunk of S2$\to$S3 likely sits in the IEEPA carve-out term. Decomposing further by HS2 chapter inside electronics / pharma / energy would tell us how much of the carve-out story is each product class.
  \item \textbf{Tracker error itemization.} Two diagnostics (\texttt{05a tracker\_miss}, \texttt{05b tracker\_over}) export operator-handoff CSVs identifying specific HTS codes where collected duties exceed (or fall short of) tracker rates. These bound how much of the residual is measurement error vs.\ behavior.
  \item \textbf{Welfare framing.} Pair our duty-collection ETR series with Waugh's TRI. The framework's per-cell rates are the inputs; estimating dispersion and the welfare-equivalent uniform rate is mechanical from there.
  \item \textbf{2018--19 vs 2025 comparison.} Eck et al.\ note the 2018 episode had a 22\% gap fraction; the 2025 episode has 44\%. Re-running the framework on 2017--2019 data would test whether the channel mix has structurally changed.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{A2. Shapley two-way derivation}

\small

For aggregate $S(w) = \sum_g s_g(w) R_g(w)$ where $g$ partitions $i = (\text{HS10}, \text{cty})$ cells, with $R_g(w) = \sum_{i \in g} w_i r_i / \sum_{i \in g} w_i$ and $s_g(w) = \sum_{i \in g} w_i / \sum_i w_i$:

\medskip

A first-order Shapley decomposition of $\Delta S = S(w^1) - S(w^0)$ averages the two orderings of (share change, rate change):
\[
\Delta S = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\sum_g\!\left[(R_g^0 + R_g^1)(s_g^1 - s_g^0)\right] + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\sum_g\!\left[(s_g^0 + s_g^1)(R_g^1 - R_g^0)\right].
\]

\medskip

Validation: across both lenses (country, product) and across all months, the per-rung sum $\sum_g \text{between}_g + \text{within}_g$ matches the framework's \texttt{gap\_diversion} from \texttt{counterfactual\_ladder.dta} to within numerical noise. This is asserted in 03 Section B at runtime.

\medskip

Sign convention: positive contribution = positive contribution to $S^{\text{2024 wts}} - S^{\text{monthly wts}}$ at the fixed rate panel.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{A3. Rate panel construction}

\small

Per (HS10 $\times$ country) cell, the tariff-rate-tracker (\texttt{src/06\_calculate\_rates.R}) produces \emph{per-revision} rates by stacking authority components:

\begin{enumerate}\small
  \item Authority components from HTSUS / Chapter 99 text: \texttt{rate\_232}, \texttt{rate\_ieepa\_recip}, \texttt{rate\_fent}, \texttt{rate\_301}, \texttt{rate\_s122}, base MFN.
  \item Metal-content scaling (S232): rate scaled by per-type metal share; IEEPA recip / S122 apply to the non-metal portion.
  \item Authority stacking: China-with-232 = $232 + \text{recip} \cdot \text{nonmetal} + \text{fent} + 301 + \text{s122} \cdot \text{nonmetal}$; CA/MX-with-232 = $232 + (\text{recip} + \text{fent} + \text{s122}) \cdot \text{nonmetal}$; without-232 = all authorities full.
  \item USMCA scaling at the H2-2025 average (or 2024 baseline / monthly empirical, depending on scenario). Auto/MHD parts use a 0.40 US-content rule.
  \item MFN exemption shares (HS2 $\times$ country) and IEEPA floor adjustments (10\% minimum for EU/JP/KR/CH).
\end{enumerate}

\medskip

Per-revision rates are then \alert{day-weighted across revisions within each month} in R section 3e to produce HS10 $\times$ country $\times$ month panels (one per USMCA scenario), so mid-month policy changes (e.g., Liberation Day on April 2, 2025) are correctly captured.

\end{frame}

S2 $\to$ S3 other prefs     & part of ``frictions'' (5.5 pp) & part of ``rate discrepancies'' \\[0.4em]
S3 $\to$ T residual+timing  & remainder of ``frictions'' (ACH, customs delays, CIT ruling) & residual rate discrepancies \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\vspace{0.6em}

Our framework's contribution: separates the (large) USMCA piece, isolates non-USMCA preferences as a distinct rung, and provides a Shapley-clean partition of trade diversion that the literature handles ad hoc.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{A9. Limitations \& caveats}

\small

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H2-2025 baseline is a stylized choice.} If USMCA claim rates continue to evolve post-Feb 2026, the S1/S2 panel anchors may need updating. The framework structure tolerates this — just rebuild \texttt{rate\_h2avg} from new tracker scenario snapshots.
  \item \textbf{Product groupings are policy-salient, not exhaustive.} The 9-group split groups copper (Ch 74) into ``Other Manufactured'' even though it became S232-relevant in June 2025. A finer split is straightforward.
  \item \textbf{Treasury timing.} The S4 $\to$ T gap mixes cash-vs-accrual, refunds, FTZ deferrals, and post-entry adjustments. Our framework does not separately attribute these; further work would require Treasury microdata.
  \item \textbf{S2 $\to$ S3 by-group decomposition is in development.} The country/product Shapley currently runs only for S1 $\to$ S2. Extending to other rungs is mechanical given the same helper infrastructure.
  \item \textbf{Pre-2025 historical comparison absent.} The framework can in principle be re-run on 2017--19 data; this is on the to-do list.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}
