# Literature Review: The Gap Between Statutory and Actual Effective Tariff Rates

## 1. Introduction

The dramatic escalation of U.S. tariff policy beginning in 2025 has generated an equally dramatic empirical puzzle: the actual effective tariff rate (ETR)—measured as duties collected divided by import value—has consistently fallen far short of the statutory rates implied by announced policy. By late 2025, the trade-weighted average statutory tariff rate had reached approximately 27 percent, the highest since the 1930s, yet the actual ETR hovered around 10–11 percent (Gopinath and Neiman, 2026; Penn Wharton Budget Model, 2026). Understanding the sources of this gap has become a central concern for trade economists, fiscal forecasters, and policymakers alike.

This review surveys the rapidly growing body of research that documents, decomposes, and theorizes about the divergence between statutory and realized tariff rates. It draws on three anchor papers—Gopinath and Neiman (2026), Azzimonti (2025), and Cui et al. (2025)—alongside complementary work from the Yale Budget Lab, the Penn Wharton Budget Model, the Minneapolis Fed, the Harvard Business School Pricing Lab, and others.

## 2. Documenting the Gap

### 2.1 Gopinath and Neiman (2026): "The Incidence of Tariffs: Rates and Reality"

This NBER working paper (w34620) provides the most comprehensive treatment of the statutory–actual tariff gap. The authors document that as of September 2025, the trade-weighted statutory rate stood at roughly 27 percent, while the actual implemented rate was approximately half that level. They attribute the gap to four main factors: shipping lags (goods already in transit when tariffs were announced remained exempt), exemptions (particularly USMCA compliance for Canadian and Mexican imports), trade agreement utilization, and enforcement issues. Critically, while the gap moderates the tariffs' immediate impact, the authors find that tariff pass-through to U.S. import prices is nearly 100 percent on actually collected tariffs—approximately 94 percent in 2025 versus 80 percent in 2018–2019—meaning that U.S. importers and consumers bear the vast majority of the costs of tariffs that are actually implemented.

### 2.2 Azzimonti (2025): "Why Predicted and Actual Tariff Rates Diverged in May 2025"

This Richmond Fed Economic Brief provides a granular, month-specific decomposition of the gap. In May 2025, the predicted average effective tariff rate (AETR) based on announced policies was 17.5 percent, while the actual AETR from Census Bureau data was just 8.7 percent—a gap of 8.8 percentage points. Azzimonti decomposes this gap into three channels:

- **Within-country product mix changes** (0.6 pp): Importers shifted away from heavily tariffed goods within each source country, but this played only a minor role.
- **Cross-country import share shifts** (2.7 pp): Imports from high-tariff countries like China fell sharply (45 percent below 2024 levels), while imports from lower-tariff sources such as Vietnam and the EU rose. This had a more meaningful but still incomplete explanatory role.
- **Implementation frictions** (~5.5 pp): The dominant factor. Many country-product pairs that should have been tariffed either generated no duties or far less than expected. This reflects legacy exemptions, shipping timing, deferred payments through automated clearinghouse systems, customs system delays, and ongoing litigation (including the May 2025 Court of International Trade ruling that blocked certain Liberation Day tariffs).

The paper's Canada case study is particularly revealing: 31 percent of Canadian product lines with positive predicted tariffs generated zero duty revenue in May. Industries like electronics (19 pp gap), electrical equipment (10 pp gap), and machinery (7 pp gap) showed especially large implementation shortfalls, while transportation equipment—where tariffs were implemented more promptly—showed almost no discrepancy.

### 2.3 Penn Wharton Budget Model (PWBM) Tracking Series

PWBM has published a series of monthly updates tracking actual ETRs against counterfactual "pre-substitution" rates. Their most recent data (through January 2026) show the effective tariff rate at 10.3 percent, up from 2.3 percent in January 2025. They decompose tariff revenue into a "mechanical" component (what would have been collected absent behavioral changes) and a "behavioral" component (revenue lost due to shifts in purchasing patterns). Through January 2026, new tariffs raised approximately $209 billion, but an additional ~$46 billion was lost to behavioral responses. PWBM also documents the surge in USMCA compliance claims, which rose from roughly 33–38 percent of Canadian and Mexican imports through 2024 to nearly 88 percent by December 2025—one of the most significant channels through which statutory rates fail to translate into actual collections.

### 2.4 Yale Budget Lab Tracking

The Yale Budget Lab has provided complementary tracking, documenting that the actual effective tariff rate was about 10 percent in June 2025, rising to approximately 11.5 percent in August, versus statutory rates of 15.7–18.2 percent in those months. Their work emphasizes the distinction between pre-substitution metrics (which capture the full cost consumers face before making adjustments) and post-substitution metrics (after import shifts), noting that the pre-substitution rate better captures the consumer welfare impact.

### 2.5 Eck, Hoang, Mix, and Ray (2026): "Mind the Gap"

This Federal Reserve Board FEDS Note (April 2026) provides the most rigorous within-episode comparison in the literature to date, decomposing the announced-versus-implied tariff gap and directly contrasting the 2025 experience with the 2018–2019 episode. By December 2025, the announced effective tariff rate (ETR) had reached 14.7 percent—a 12.4 percentage-point increase over 2024—while the realized effective tariff rate (RETR), computed from duties actually collected, fell 5.43 pp short, or roughly 44 percent of the announced ETR increase. The corresponding 2018–2019 gap was just 0.44 pp (22 percent of the announced increase by December 2018), despite that earlier episode being far more concentrated (Herfindahl–Hirschman Index of 0.29, primarily China-focused) than the 2025 regime (HHI 0.06, broadly distributed).

The authors decompose the gap into two channels:

- **Composition effects**: shifts in import sourcing toward lower-tariff products and countries, including anticipatory frontloading, tariff engineering (reclassifying into lower-rate categories), supplier diversification, and possible misclassification or under-invoicing. Crucially, in 2025 these shifts began *immediately* after tariff announcements, in contrast to the slow, gradual substitution observed in 2018–2019—which the authors interpret as evidence of "more agile supply chains" post-COVID.
- **Rate discrepancies**: at the country-product level, actual duties diverge from announced rates due to shipment-timing lags, rising USMCA compliance, and free-trade-zone or bonded-warehouse usage.

Methodologically, the announced ETR is computed as country-product tariff rates weighted by baseline import shares from a reference year (2017 for the 2018–2019 episode; 2024 for 2025), and the realized rate as customs duties collected divided by customs value of imports; the decomposition is then a Laspeyres-style separation of composition shifts from per-cell rate discrepancies. Anchoring to fixed baseline weights isolates behavioral responses cleanly.

The authors conclude that the gap is likely transitory if tariffs persist—frontloaded inventories will deplete, shipment-timing effects will dissipate—but that permanent supply-chain restructuring may sustain a portion of it, given firms' experience diversifying suppliers after 2018–2019 and COVID. The implication is that the 44 percent gap represents an upper bound that should compress over time, but likely not back to the 22 percent baseline of 2018–2019.

## 3. Measurement Debates: How to Summarize the Tariff Regime

### 3.1 Waugh (2026): The Trade Restrictiveness Index

An important methodological contribution comes from Michael Waugh of the Minneapolis Fed, who argues that both the statutory average and the duties-to-imports ratio understate the true economic burden of the current tariff regime. Drawing on the theoretical framework of Anderson and Neary (1996), Waugh computes a Trade Restrictiveness Index (TRI)—the uniform tariff rate that would produce the same welfare cost as the actual, highly uneven tariff structure. As of October–November 2025, the TRI stood at approximately 21–23 percent, roughly twice the ~10.5 percent effective rate calculated by PWBM and about 24 percent higher than the statutory average from the Yale Budget Lab. The key insight is that the welfare cost of tariffs grows exponentially (with the square of the rate), so a few very high tariff rates cause disproportionately more economic harm than many moderate ones at the same average level. This is particularly relevant given that the 2025 U.S. tariff structure features wide dispersion, with rates on China exceeding 100 percent while many goods enter duty-free under USMCA or other exemptions.

### 3.2 Implications for Measurement

The measurement debate highlights that different ETR concepts serve different purposes. The duties-to-imports ratio captures actual fiscal impact and near-term price effects; the statutory or "predicted" rate captures announced policy intent; the pre-substitution weighted average captures the full price shock consumers face before behavioral adjustment; and the TRI captures the welfare-equivalent cost accounting for tariff dispersion. The gap between statutory and actual rates should therefore be understood as reflecting not just measurement error or implementation delays, but genuine economic responses (trade diversion, USMCA utilization) that are features, not bugs, of the tariff system—though the extent to which they represent intended versus unintended consequences remains debated.

## 4. Sources of the Gap: A Taxonomy

Drawing across the literature, the sources of the statutory–actual gap can be organized into four categories:

### 4.1 Implementation Frictions and Timing

This is consistently identified as the largest source of the gap in the early months. Gopinath and Neiman (2026) emphasize shipping lags (goods already on the water when tariffs are announced), while Azzimonti (2025) adds deferred payment mechanisms (up to six weeks for ACH-enrolled importers) and delays in customs system updates. Azzimonti finds that for Canada, many high-value product lines—such as aircraft, turbojets, and chemical woodpulp—had zero duty collections despite being subject to 25 percent tariffs on paper. These frictions are expected to be largely transitory, and the literature generally predicts convergence over time. The PWBM data confirm partial convergence: the effective rate rose from about 8.7 percent in May to 10.9 percent by October 2025.

### 4.2 Exemptions and Trade Agreement Utilization

USMCA compliance has proven to be one of the most quantitatively significant channels. PWBM data show that the share of Canadian and Mexican imports claiming USMCA exemptions surged from ~33 percent to ~88 percent within a year. The Yale Budget Lab has noted that even beyond USMCA, a meaningful share of imports from Canada and Mexico enter on tariff-free authorities such as MFN zero-rate lines—a finding that led them to revise their methodology, lowering estimated effective rates on Canada by 6 pp and Mexico by nearly 3 pp. This channel represents a mix of pre-existing exemptions becoming more economically relevant and active behavioral adjustment by firms to exploit available duty-free pathways.

### 4.3 Trade Diversion and Sourcing Changes

Azzimonti (2025) documents sharp declines in imports from China (down 45 percent relative to 2024 shares) alongside increases from Vietnam and the EU. The broader literature on trade reallocation—including Alfaro and Chor (2025) on the "Great Reallocation"—confirms that the 2025 tariffs accelerated a sourcing diversification trend already underway since 2018–2019. Firms responding to the Richmond Fed CFO Survey reported plans to diversify supply chains (27 percent) and move up purchases (24.6 percent). However, Azzimonti finds that cross-country substitution accounts for only about 2.7 pp of the 8.8 pp gap in May 2025, making it the second-most important but not the dominant factor.

### 4.4 Legal and Regulatory Uncertainty

The May 2025 Court of International Trade ruling blocking "Liberation Day" tariffs under IEEPA introduced substantial legal uncertainty and likely caused pauses in enforcement. The subsequent Supreme Court case and the eventual transition from IEEPA tariffs to Section 122 authority further complicated the statutory landscape. PWBM has estimated that removing IEEPA tariffs would reduce the effective rate to approximately 4.4 percent, illustrating how much of the statutory regime was vulnerable to legal challenge.

## 5. Firm-Level Responses and Pass-Through

### 5.1 Cui, Feng, Lu, and Tomlin (2025): Strategic Responses to Tariff Risk

This SSRN paper (No. 5110124) provides an analytical framework for understanding how firms optimize their responses to tariff uncertainty. The authors distinguish between mitigation strategies (stockpiling, sourcing diversification—implemented proactively) and contingency strategies (responsive pricing—deployed reactively). A key finding is that under mitigation strategies, price increases are driven primarily by the *probability* of tariffs rather than the tariff rate itself, and price increases are less pronounced than under contingency strategies. As tariff risk escalates or competition intensifies, firms shift from contingency to mitigation strategies. Sourcing diversification emerges as the more sustainable long-term strategy compared with stockpiling. When tariff rates are uncertain, partial mitigation strategies allow firms to avoid paying high-rate tariffs while incurring limited costs under low-rate scenarios. This theoretical framework helps explain why the gap between statutory and actual ETRs may be partially structural: firms that have diversified sources or built inventory are effectively insulated from the full statutory rate.

### 5.2 Pass-Through Evidence

The question of how tariffs that are actually collected transmit to prices is closely related to the gap question. Cavallo, Llamas, and Vazquez (2025) use daily retail microdata from the Harvard Pricing Lab to track pass-through of the 2025 tariffs. They find that retail tariff pass-through was approximately 20 percent after six months—higher than the under-5 percent observed in 2018–2019, but still incomplete—contributing about 0.7 percentage points to CPI inflation by September 2025. Importantly, they note that the size of price effects relative to *applied* (not statutory) tariff rates is more meaningful, and the difference between statutory and applied rates complicates pass-through measurement.

At the border level, Gopinath and Neiman (2026) find near-complete pass-through of *actually collected* tariffs to duty-inclusive import prices. This apparent contradiction—near-complete border pass-through but incomplete retail pass-through—reflects the role of distribution margins, markups, and the gradual transmission of border costs through multi-tier supply chains, as explored in Flaaen, Hortaçsu, Tintelnot, Urdaneta, and Xu (2025) using wine industry data.

The Yale Budget Lab estimates implied passthrough of tariffs to imported consumer goods prices at roughly 40–76 percent for core goods and 47–106 percent for durables through December 2025, depending on methodology, noting that these are descriptive estimates rather than causal identification.

## 6. Historical Context and Long-Run Perspective

EFG International (Gerlach, 2025) places the current episode in a 150-year context, noting that the effective tariff rate can be computed back to 1867, when customs duties approached 50 percent as the federal government's main revenue source. The post-WWII era saw a long secular decline to under 2 percent, a trend reversed by the 2018–2019 tariffs and dramatically accelerated in 2025. The current effective rate of roughly 10–12 percent, while far below the statutory ceiling, is nonetheless the highest in nearly a century. This historical lens highlights that the gap between statutory and effective rates is not a new phenomenon—in the late 19th century, tariffs were concentrated on goods that were nonetheless still imported, and firms have always responded to tariff structures through product substitution and sourcing adjustments. What is novel about the 2025 episode is the speed and scale of both the tariff changes and the behavioral responses, as well as the administrative complexity of implementing tariffs across thousands of product-country pairs simultaneously.

## 7. Gaps in the Literature and Directions for Future Research

Several important questions remain underexplored:

1. **Convergence dynamics**: Will the statutory–actual gap narrow over time as implementation frictions resolve, or will behavioral responses (USMCA utilization, trade diversion) keep it persistently wide? The early evidence suggests partial convergence—the gap narrowed from May to October 2025—but full convergence is unlikely given the structural nature of many exemptions.

2. **Welfare implications of the gap**: The gap moderates the near-term fiscal and price impacts of tariffs, but it also reflects real economic costs (the resources firms devote to tariff avoidance, the efficiency losses from trade diversion to potentially less efficient suppliers). Waugh's TRI approach begins to capture some of these costs, but a full welfare accounting remains incomplete.

3. **Tariff engineering and evasion**: Flaaen et al. (2025) document tariff engineering in the wine industry, where firms adjusted product characteristics to avoid duties. The extent to which similar strategies are being employed across industries in the 2025 episode is not yet well documented.

4. **Dynamic firm-level responses**: While Cui et al. (2025) provide a theoretical framework and Barbiero, Silva, Sheremirov, and Stein (2025) examine firm-level financial impacts, the interaction between firm-level strategies (stockpiling, sourcing, pricing) and the aggregate statutory–actual gap deserves further empirical investigation.

5. **Legal uncertainty channel**: The impact of ongoing litigation—including Supreme Court cases on IEEPA authority and the transition to Section 122 tariffs—on both firm behavior and customs enforcement has not been formally modeled.

6. **Cross-country comparison**: The current literature is overwhelmingly focused on U.S. tariffs. Whether similar statutory–actual gaps appear in retaliating countries (particularly China) and in other trade policy regimes could provide valuable comparative evidence.

---

## 8. Conclusion

The gap between statutory and actual effective tariff rates has emerged as one of the defining empirical features of the 2025 U.S. trade policy episode. At its peak, actual rates were roughly half of statutory rates—a discrepancy driven primarily by implementation frictions, USMCA and other exemptions, trade diversion, and legal uncertainty. The literature documents this gap from multiple angles: macro-level tracking (PWBM, Yale Budget Lab), detailed product-country decomposition (Azzimonti), border and retail price analysis (Gopinath and Neiman; Cavallo et al.), firm-level strategy frameworks (Cui et al.), and welfare-equivalent measurement (Waugh). Collectively, this body of work makes clear that announced tariff rates are a poor guide to actual trade costs, and that understanding the transmission from policy to implementation requires attention to administrative capacity, firm behavior, legal constraints, and the structure of trade agreements.
