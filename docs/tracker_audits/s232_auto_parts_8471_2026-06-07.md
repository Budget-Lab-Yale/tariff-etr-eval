# Audit memo: HTS 8471 and the Section 232 auto-parts applicability fix

**Date:** 2026-06-07
**Tracker fix:** `c471770` ("Scope 232 auto parts to vehicle parts"), part of the 2026-06-04
methodology fix pass. This memo documents the original miss, the statutory basis for the
correction, and why the corrected treatment is the more appropriate reading of the law.

## Summary

Through the 2026-06-04 fix pass, the tracker applied the Section 232 automobile-parts duty
(heading 9903.94.05, 25%) to **all** entries classified under HTS heading 8471 — automatic
data processing machines, i.e. laptops, desktops, servers, and storage units — because 8471
appears on the list of covered HTS provisions in U.S. note 33(g) to subchapter III of
chapter 99. After semiconductor-qualifying-share scaling this produced an effective
`rate_232` of ~23.8% on roughly $139B/yr of (mostly Taiwanese) computer imports. Census
collections on these lines show essentially no Section 232 duty (~0.8% collected vs 13.5%
statutory on Taiwan ch84 over 2025m5–2026m2). The fix excludes 8471 from the auto-parts
duty via the new per-heading applicability share, and was the single largest contributor to
the post-April-2025 collapse of the statutory-vs-declared residual in the ETR decomposition
(−0.48pp GTAP-weighted statutory ETR; larger under Census weights).

## The miss

The list in note 33(g) is real: bare heading **8471** is enumerated among the auto-parts
provisions, in the original annex to Proclamation 10908 as published at 90 FR 14705, 14712
(Apr. 3, 2025) — between `8431.10.0090` and `8482.10.10`. It is one of only two bare
4-digit headings on the list (the other is 8707, vehicle bodies); nearly every other entry
is an 8- or 10-digit automotive-specific provision (e.g. `8409.91.1040` engine parts,
`8536.41.0005` automotive relays). The tracker's parser treated list membership as
sufficient for the duty to apply, which is the correct convention for most chapter-99
programs (301 lists, 232 metals annexes) — but not for this one.

## Why the corrected treatment is the better statutory reading

**1. The note conditions the duty on the article being a vehicle part — in the same
sentence that introduces the list.** Note 33(g) (90 FR 14712):

> "The rates of duty set forth in heading 9903.94.05 applies to **parts of passenger
> vehicles** (sedans, sport utility vehicles, crossover utility vehicles, minivans, and
> cargo vans) **and light trucks** classifiable in the provisions of the HTSUS enumerated
> in this subdivision: …"

The enumerated provisions define the candidate classification space; the duty attaches only
to articles within that space that *are* parts of passenger vehicles and light trucks. A
data-center server classified in 8471 is inside the listed provisions but outside the
duty's article description.

**2. The zero-duty bucket for non-vehicle-part articles is explicit, not inferred.**
Note 33(h) and heading 9903.94.06 (90 FR 14713):

> "(h) Heading 9903.94.06 applies to all entries of articles classifiable under provisions
> of the HTSUS enumerated [in] subdivision (g) of this note (i) that are eligible for
> special tariff treatment under the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA), other
> than automobile knock-down kits or parts compilations; or (ii) **that are not parts of
> passenger vehicles … and light trucks**."

9903.94.06 carries no additional duty ("the duty provided in the applicable subheading").
The drafters anticipated that the listed provisions sweep in non-automotive articles and
built the relief valve into the HTS text itself. CBP's filing guidance (CSMS #64913145,
May 2025) and its Section 232 autos FAQ instruct exactly this two-bucket reporting.

**3. The Section 232 predicate cannot support a blanket computer tariff.** Proclamation
10908 ¶1 recites that the underlying 2019 investigation covered "certain automobile parts
(**engines and engine parts, transmissions and powertrain parts, and electrical
components**)," and note 33(f) defines "automobile parts, for this purpose" in the same
terms. Section 232 authority extends to adjusting imports of the articles found to threaten
national security; general-purpose computers were never part of that finding. The
conditioned reading keeps heading 9903.94.05 within the proclamation's legal basis. The
proclamation's separate clause-(7) inclusions process (BIS interim final rule, 90 FR,
Sept. 17, 2025) exists precisely because adding *new* article scope requires a Commerce
determination — it is not accomplished by listing a broad heading in 33(g). The likely
drafting intent of listing bare 8471 is to reach genuine in-vehicle computing units
(ECUs/domain controllers classifiable in 8471), not the heading's full trade base.

**4. Collections data confirm the narrow reading is the operative one.** Census IMDB
declared duties on 8471 lines show ~no Section 232 collections (Taiwan ch84: 0.8% collected
vs 13.5% old-tracker statutory on $139B of trade, 2025m5–2026m2). Importers file
9903.94.06 on non-automotive 8471 entries, and CBP accepts it. A statutory series intended
to measure "what the law requires importers to pay" should reflect the duty's legal scope,
not the union of its candidate classifications.

## What the fix does

`c471770` adds an `applicability_shares_file` to the 232 auto-parts heading config. The
interim calibration sets 8471's applicability to zero (Census-supported); other listed
provisions keep applicability 1.0 pending evidence they carry material non-automotive
trade. The proper long-run treatment is an empirical applicability share per dual-use
provision (8471 genuinely contains *some* vehicle parts), but at current evidence the share
is statistically indistinguishable from zero in the aggregate.

## Effect on the ETR evaluation

In the three-model decomposition (`tariff-rate-tracker/output/model_compare/`,
2026-06-06), this fix contributes −0.48pp to the GTAP-weighted statutory ETR at every
sampled revision from rev_18 (Aug 2025) through 2026_rev_2 (Feb 2026) — the largest single
channel in the B−A (policy-fix) effect. Because the auto-parts duty took effect May 3,
2025, the overstatement begins in 2025m5, which is why the eval's statutory-vs-declared
residual collapse is concentrated in the post-April-2025 window. Under the eval's
Census-weighted panel the contribution is larger still (8471 is among the largest
import lines by value).

## Open follow-up (annex era, April 2026+)

With 8471 removed from `heading_program_products`, the step-5c metals-annex override now
binds from `2026_rev_5`: bare 8471 is on annex I-B (aluminum) per the April 2026
proclamation's own note 16(c) list, so production charges computers 25% full-value
annex_1b from Apr 6, 2026 (vs 23.76% before — levels barely moved). Same dual-use issue,
new legal vehicle; no post-April-2026 Census evidence yet (eval window ends 2026m3).
Decision deferred until April+ collections land — the dormant 9903.82.01 zero-metal-content
carve-out or an annex-level applicability share are the natural knobs. See
`tariff-rate-tracker/todo.md` item 2(a).
